Engineering the Strait

Every Eid, the Merak–Bakauheni ferry crossing — Indonesia's busiest, just 15 nautical miles of water between Java and Sumatra — buckles under demand it cannot physically carry. This is how an optimization-and-simulation model turns that gridlock into a five-day backup-port plan, and what it costs to keep traffic moving.

01 / The chokepoint

A 15-mile crossing the whole archipelago leans on

The Merak–Bakauheni route is the land bridge between Java and Sumatra. In 2024 it ran 65 vessels and roughly 128 departures a day. On an ordinary day the Merak terminal sits at about 46% of capacity — comfortable.

Then comes Lebaran.

02 / Anatomy of a peak

Demand doesn't just rise. It breaks the ceiling.

Projected for the 2025 Eid exodus, daily load climbs from H-7 and crosses the Ministry of Transport's safe operating line — a Vehicle-to-Capacity (V/C) ratio of 0.80 — on H-5. By H-2 it hits 115%: more vehicles want to cross than the port can physically clear in a day.

Everything above the dashed line is a queue that spills into tomorrow.

V/C ratio = projected vehicle load (in passenger-car units, SMP) ÷ daily terminal capacity. Above 0.80 is officially "over standard."
03 / What's actually in the queue

Cars are the weight. Motorcycles are the count.

The 27,964-SMP peak hides a split personality. By volume of load, passenger cars (Class IV) are roughly three-quarters of the problem. By sheer number of units, motorcycles (Class II) dominate — over 16,000 of them, each taking little space but all needing a slot.

That distinction is the hinge of the whole solution: you can't treat every vehicle the same.

The Approach

Two engines, one objective

The policy already existed — since 2022 the Ministry has diverted overflow to "backup ports." But it was set by intuition: long jams persisted, and shipping firms were forced onto longer routes with no compensation. The question this project answers is sharper: which ports, for which vehicles, with which ships — at minimum cost to the state and minimum pain to the public.

04 / The model

Optimize the plan, then simulate the consequence

An optimization model (solved with an evolutionary solver) chooses the cheapest feasible plan. Its objective: minimize total compensation cost. Its levers: which port-pair schemes to activate, which vehicle classes to divert, and which vessels to assign. Its hard constraint: keep every port's V/C ratio at or below 0.80.

A separate queueing simulation — Poisson arrivals feeding a waiting-time model — then measures what that plan actually does to the queue, before versus after.

05 / The candidates

Five ports auditioned. Each had to pass.

Nearby terminals were screened against a compatibility checklist — berth type, draft, yard area, road access. Five viable candidates emerged, combined into five operating schemes (A–E), each a specific origin→destination pairing for a specific kind of vehicle.

Not all would make the final cut. The model decides.

06 / The optimized plan

Split the load three ways, and Merak breathes

The solver keeps passenger cars (IV, VA, VII) at Merak but adds capacity through Scheme A (Indah Kiat). It sends motorcycles and one truck class (II, VIA) to Scheme D (Ciwandan), and the heaviest cargo (VB, VIB, VIII, IX) to Scheme E (Bandar Bakau Jaya).

Merak's V/C ratio falls from a gridlocked 1.15 to 0.80. Every backup port lands under the line too.

11 existing RoRo vessels reassigned — 4 to Scheme A, 7 to Scheme D — plus 6 of Bandar Bakau Jaya's own LCT craft on Scheme E. No new ships chartered.

The Ledger

What it costs, and what it buys back

A plan that keeps every port under capacity is only worth running if the public gains more than the state spends. So the model puts both sides of the trade on the same scale: rupiah out in compensation, rupiah saved in avoided gridlock.

07 / The bill

Rp 17.8 billion to run it for five days

Rp 17.77 B

Compensation splits in a revealing way: 83% goes to shipping companies (fuel for diverted vessels, plus payments to ships displaced by the reshuffle) and only 17% to port operators.

Scheme E costs the state nothing — the Bandar Bakau Jaya fare is already cheaper than the regular crossing, so no top-up is owed.

08 / The relief

From a five-hour wait to under two

This is the payoff the public feels. On the worst day, average waiting time falls from 5h 17m to 1h 58m. The true pre-policy maximum — 6h 11m on H-1, swollen by yesterday's spillover — drops to 1h 45m.

Even on Eid day itself, after operations end, the relief lingers: there's no backlog left to clear. Average reduction across the peak: 68%.

09 / The verdict

Every rupiah spent saves the public 3.4

Avoided gridlock — wasted fuel plus lost travel time — comes to Rp 59.6 billion. Against the Rp 17.8 billion price tag, that's a benefit-to-cost ratio of 3.4.

Most of that value is indirect: the hours people don't lose sitting in line (BCR 2.73) far outweigh the fuel they don't burn (BCR 0.63).

The Honest Part

A bridge, not a destination

The most useful finding isn't the savings. It's the expiry date.

10 / The lever

How hard you push the standard sets the price

The single biggest cost driver isn't the ports — it's how strictly you hold the V/C line. Insist on 0.75 and the plan must reach all the way to distant Pelabuhan Panjang with chartered ships: cost balloons to Rp 66 billion. Relax to 0.95 and it shrinks to Rp 3 billion.

The chosen 0.80 sits at a deliberate knee in the curve: safe, but not ruinously so.

11 / The expiry

This plan works… until roughly 2028

Run the same model against future demand and the cost climbs relentlessly. Through 2027 the 0.80 standard still holds — but only by chartering more and more ships. By 2028 it can no longer hold 0.80 at all; the standard has to be loosened year after year.

By 2033, Class IV demand alone exceeds Merak's capacity. No clever diversion fixes that.

The backup-port policy is a high-return stopgap that buys time — not a substitute for new berth infrastructure. Naming that ceiling is the point.
Fig · Durability

The takeaway

A backup-port policy, properly optimized, is one of the cheapest interventions available to a congested ferry system: Rp 17.8 billion to cut peak waiting times by two-thirds and return Rp 3.4 of public value per rupiah spent. But its returns decay fast. Treated as a bridge to capacity expansion it is excellent value; treated as a permanent answer, it quietly fails within a decade.
